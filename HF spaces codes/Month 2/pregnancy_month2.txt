[Key Developments] 

- The embryo continues to grow rapidly, now measuring about 1 inch by the end of week 8.
- Major organs and body systems begin forming from the three primary cell layers.
- The heart, which began beating in month 1, now beats regularly and can sometimes be detected by ultrasound.
- Facial features like eyes, nose, and mouth start taking shape.
- Limb buds lengthen into small arms and legs with early fingers and toes.
- The neural tube fully closes, forming the brain and spinal cord.
- Early brain regions begin differentiating — forebrain, midbrain, and hindbrain.
- The placenta continues to develop and begins taking over nutrient transfer from the yolk sac.
- The umbilical cord becomes fully functional, carrying oxygen and nutrients to the embryo.
- Internal organs such as the liver, kidneys, lungs, and stomach start forming.
- The digestive tract begins developing basic shape and position.
- The embryo’s cartilage framework starts forming — the basis for bones.
- Eyes darken as pigment develops in the retina.
- Ears begin forming both internally and externally.
- The baby’s blood circulation becomes more complex and efficient.
- The reproductive organs start forming but are not yet distinguishable by ultrasound.
- Tiny movements begin as muscles develop, though they aren’t felt yet.
- The head is large compared to the body due to rapid brain growth.
- Tiny nostrils and eyelids appear but remain closed.
- Hands and feet form webbed digits that will later separate.
- The embryo’s heartbeat can range from 120–160 beats per minute.
- The spinal cord extends and nerves begin branching out.
- The body starts to straighten from its initial curved position.
- The amniotic sac expands, filled with protective fluid cushioning the embryo.
- Major organ systems like the heart, brain, and digestive tract are now in place.
- The yolk sac gradually shrinks as the placenta assumes full function.
- The embryo now resembles a tiny human more closely than before.
- Blood begins circulating between the embryo and placenta.
- The baby’s size grows from about a sesame seed to a raspberry.
- Facial features like the upper lip and lower jaw take form.
- The baby’s neural development continues at lightning speed.
- The baby’s skin is extremely thin and translucent.
- Cells begin specializing into tissues for bones, nerves, and muscles.
- Early signs of eyelids cover developing eyes for protection.
- The brain produces early electrical activity detectable by instruments.
- The liver starts producing blood cells.
- The digestive tract connects from the mouth to the developing stomach and intestines.
- The baby’s heartbeat may now be visible on an early ultrasound scan.
- Month 2 marks the end of the “embryonic” period — after this, it’s called a fetus.
- The foundation for all major organs and limbs is complete by the end of this month.

 
[Common Symptoms] 

- Morning sickness usually intensifies due to high hCG levels.
- Nausea may occur any time of day, not just in the morning.
- Vomiting can start, especially with strong odors or certain foods.
- Fatigue remains high as the body works on organ formation.
- Breasts feel fuller, tender, or more sensitive.
- Nipples darken further, and small bumps (Montgomery glands) may appear.
- Mood swings continue due to rising progesterone and estrogen.
- Mild abdominal cramps or pulling sensations from uterine stretching.
- Bloating and indigestion worsen as digestion slows.
- Increased urination continues as the uterus presses on the bladder.
- Food aversions may grow stronger.
- Heightened sense of smell persists, often triggering nausea.
- Constipation and gas may become more noticeable.
- Mild headaches may occur due to hormone changes or dehydration.
- Dizziness or lightheadedness from lower blood pressure is common.
- Skin changes such as oiliness or acne may begin or worsen.
- Vaginal discharge may increase — clear or white is normal.
- Mild weight changes — some lose a bit from nausea, others gain slightly.
- Temperature may remain elevated.
- Metallic taste may persist.
- Some experience excess saliva (ptyalism).
- Emotional sensitivity and weepiness remain common.
- Sleep disturbances may start due to discomfort or vivid dreams.
- Some women notice a slight baby bump or bloating belly.
- Mild pelvic pressure as uterus expands.
- Occasional mild spotting can still occur but should be monitored.
- Appetite may fluctuate daily — sometimes little interest, sometimes intense hunger.
- Skin pigmentation may slightly darken (early melasma).
- Shortness of breath during mild activity due to blood volume increase.
- Increased thirst and need for hydration.
- Some experience mild heartburn or reflux.
- A feeling of warmth or sweating more than usual.
- Sharp smells like perfume, soap, or cooking may trigger nausea.
- Some may notice visible veins on breasts or abdomen.
- Every pregnancy continues to differ widely — all symptom variations can be normal.

- Wear loose clothing to prevent belly discomfort.
- Choose soft, non-restrictive bras for breast tenderness.

- Keep salt intake moderate to prevent swelling.
- Prioritize 7–9 hours of sleep nightly.
- Schedule your first prenatal visit if not already done.
- Note all medications and confirm safety with your doctor.

- Avoid processed and junk foods for stable energy.
- Monitor any spotting — inform your doctor if heavy.
- Use relaxation methods like slow breathing to reduce stress.
- Keep a journal of symptoms to share at appointments.
- Drink water before bedtime to reduce early dehydration.
- Begin thinking about prenatal checkups and blood tests.
- Stay positive — development this month is critical and fast.

 

[Medical Guidance] 

- Attend your first prenatal appointment during this month.
- Your doctor will confirm pregnancy via ultrasound or hCG tests.
- Blood tests may check blood type, Rh factor, and infections.
- A baseline blood pressure and weight record will be taken.
- Urine tests check protein and glucose levels.
- Ask about prenatal screening options for genetic health.
- The first ultrasound may show the heartbeat around week 6–8.
- Discuss morning sickness management — possible B6 or medication.
- Inform your doctor about severe nausea or dehydration (possible hyperemesis).
- Ask about safe exercise and dietary supplements.
- Discuss any existing medical conditions like thyroid or diabetes.
- Ask for guidance about travel safety and work precautions.
- Report any heavy bleeding, severe cramps, or dizziness immediately.
- Ensure dental checkups are current — gum infections can impact pregnancy.
- Ask if you need vaccinations (like flu shot or Tdap later).
- If Rh-negative, your doctor will plan future Rh factor management.
- Ask about safe skincare and haircare products.
- Check your iron and folate levels.
- Discuss emotional wellbeing and mental health openly.
- If carrying twins or multiples, special monitoring may start.
- Confirm your expected due date from last menstrual period.
- Ask about early nutritional needs specific to your body type.
- Share your family medical history for early screening.
- Discuss nausea-safe medications if over-the-counter options fail.
- Ask for guidance on what to avoid at work or home.
- Set a schedule for regular prenatal visits (monthly for now).
- Talk about safe sexual activity guidelines.
- Report any symptoms like burning urination, fever, or fainting.
- Keep track of blood test results and bring them to all visits.
- Trust your healthcare provider for tailored advice.

 

[Precautions] 

- Avoid alcohol, smoking, and all recreational drugs.
- Stay clear of secondhand smoke.
- Do not take any medicine unless prescribed.
- Avoid raw or undercooked meats, seafood, and eggs.
- Avoid unpasteurized dairy and juices.
- Limit caffeine to 1 small cup per day.
- Do not lift heavy objects.
- Avoid cleaning cat litter (toxoplasmosis risk).
- Avoid high-mercury fish (swordfish, king mackerel, shark).
- Stay away from chemical fumes, paint, and solvents.
- Avoid saunas, steam rooms, or overheating baths.
- Wear seatbelt correctly — under belly and across shoulder.
- Avoid energy drinks and sugary sodas.
- Wash hands frequently.
- Avoid unwashed fruits and vegetables.
- Limit screen time before bed to improve sleep.
- Do not starve yourself — even light meals are important.
- Avoid risky travel or areas without medical access.
- Avoid lying flat for long hours to reduce dizziness.
- Keep distance from sick people to avoid infections.
- Avoid stress triggers and conflict.
- Do not self-medicate for nausea or pain.
- Avoid strong perfumes or cleaning sprays.
- Limit processed meats unless fully cooked.
- Be careful with hair dyes or treatments.
- Stay away from loud or chaotic environments.
- Avoid dehydration and overheating.
- Report any severe pain or bleeding immediately.

[When to Take or Retake Pregnancy Tests] 

- By month 2, hCG levels are high enough for accurate testing anytime.
- A positive test now almost always confirms pregnancy.
- If results were unclear last month, retest now with morning urine.
- Faint lines will be stronger as hCG rises.
- Digital tests will clearly display “pregnant.”
- Store tests properly before use for accurate readings.
- Confirm pregnancy with a doctor — next step is ultrasound.
- Do not keep retesting daily; focus on medical confirmation.
- If symptoms stop suddenly, discuss with your provider.
- Keep early test results for your medical record.

 

[FAQ]

-  How big is my baby now?  About the size of a raspberry (around 1 inch).
-  Can the heartbeat be seen?  Often by week 6–8 via ultrasound.
-  Is nausea normal?  Very common — peaks this month.
-  Can I take nausea medicine?  Only with doctor’s approval.
-  Should I be gaining weight?  Minimal — 0.5 to 1.5 kg is typical.
-  What foods help nausea?  Crackers, toast, ginger, and small snacks.
-  Is mild spotting okay?  Yes, but report heavy bleeding.
-  Can I travel?  Yes, with doctor’s consent and proper rest.
-  Can I exercise?  Light and moderate exercise is safe.
-  Are mood swings normal?  Yes — hormone surges are intense.
-  Can I dye my hair?  Wait until after first trimester if possible.
-  What should I eat more of?  Iron, folate, protein, and fiber.
-  Why am I so tired?  Body is forming major organs — energy demands are high.
-  Can I still sleep on my stomach?  Still possible now but less comfortable soon.
-  When is the first ultrasound?  Around week 7–8.
-  Why do smells bother me so much?  Hormonal changes heighten senses.
-  Is heartburn common now?  Yes, as the uterus grows.
-  Can I drink tea?  Herbal teas only if doctor-approved.
-  Is it normal to worry a lot?  Yes — talk with your doctor for reassurance.
-  When will nausea end?  Usually after week 12.
