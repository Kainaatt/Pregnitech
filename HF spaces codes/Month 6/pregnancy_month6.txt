[Key Developments]
- The baby's skin is translucent and pinkish-red, covered in a protective waxy coating called vernix.
- Vernix caseosa protects the baby's skin from the amniotic fluid.
- Fine, soft hair called lanugo covers the baby's entire body to help hold the vernix on the skin.
- The baby practices breathing movements, inhaling and exhaling amniotic fluid.
- The lungs are developing surfactant, a substance that will help them inflate after birth.
- The baby's eyebrows and eyelashes are now visible.
- The fetus has a well-developed sense of hearing and can recognize your voice.
- The baby may respond to loud noises outside the womb with movement.
- Taste buds are forming, and flavors from your diet can be tasted in the amniotic fluid.
- The brain is undergoing a period of rapid growth.
- The baby's bones are continuing to harden and become stronger.
- The baby is becoming more proportional, with the head now less oversized compared to the body.
- The baby's sleep and wake cycles are becoming more regular.
- You may notice patterns of activity and rest throughout the day.
- The baby's movements are now strong and can be felt from the outside.
- Your partner can often feel the baby kick by placing a hand on your belly.
- The baby's fingerprints and footprints are now fully formed and unique.
- The baby's eyelids begin to part, and eyes can open and close.
- The baby may suck on its thumb or fingers, strengthening the sucking reflex.
- The baby's kicks and rolls may be visible from the outside, watching your belly move.
- The baby is gaining weight steadily, starting to look more like a newborn.
- Baby's approximate length reaches about 10-12 inches (crown to heel).
- Baby's approximate weight reaches about 1-1.5 pounds.
- The uterus is now the size of a soccer ball.
- Your center of gravity shifts significantly as the belly grows.
- The baby's bone marrow starts to take over production of blood cells.
- The baby's kicks can be felt under the ribs, in the hips, and all around.
- The baby may hiccup, felt as rhythmic, tiny jerks in your belly.
- All major organs are now present and continuing to mature.
- The baby's skin is still wrinkled but will smooth out as fat accumulates.
- The reproductive system continues to develop (testes descend for boys, ovaries contain eggs for girls).
- The baby's heartbeat is now strong enough to be heard with a stethoscope.
- Neural connections are forming rapidly, enhancing the baby's control over movements.
- The baby can make grasping motions and touch its own face.
- Amniotic fluid is at a peak volume, allowing the baby to move freely.
- The baby can perceive light, even through your closed eyelids.
- Baby's hair on the scalp may be growing in.
- The pancreas is developing, important for hormone production.
- The baby's body is beginning to store vital minerals like iron and calcium.
- By the end of this month, the baby is considered viable, meaning it has a chance of survival outside the womb.

[Common Symptoms]
- Backaches become more common due to the shifting center of gravity.
- Round ligament pain presents as sharp or dull aches in the lower abdomen or groin.
- Braxton Hicks contractions may become more noticeable (painless uterine tightening).
- Shortness of breath occurs as the uterus pushes up against the diaphragm.
- Heartburn and indigestion are frequent due to pressure on the stomach.
- Swelling in the ankles and feet (edema) is common, especially after standing.
- Increased appetite as the baby goes through a growth spurt.
- Stretch marks may appear on the abdomen, breasts, thighs, or buttocks.
- The belly button may "pop out" and become an outie.
- Skin changes include a linea nigra (dark line down the abdomen) and darker nipples.
- Itchy skin, particularly on the growing belly.
- Clumsiness or balance issues due to changes in posture and ligaments.
- Leg cramps, especially at night.
- Varicose veins or hemorrhoids may develop due to increased blood volume and pressure.
- A noticeable "pregnancy waddle" may develop due to pelvic changes.
- Difficulty sleeping (insomnia) due to discomfort and finding a good position.
- Nasal congestion and nosebleeds from increased blood flow to mucous membranes.
- Bleeding gums are common; practice good dental hygiene.
- Forgetfulness or "pregnancy brain" continues.
- Increased vaginal discharge (leukorrhea) is normal.
- Skin may feel warmer due to increased metabolism and blood flow.
- Hair may feel thicker and look shinier due to hormonal changes.
- Increased sweating is common.
- Mild swelling in the hands may occur.
- Feelings of warmth or hot flashes.
- Sharp, shooting pains in the vagina or cervix (lightning crotch) from baby's position.
- Pelvic pressure and achiness as ligaments loosen.
- Difficulty bending over and tying shoes.
- Lower abdominal achiness after long periods of walking or standing.
- Increased urination continues as the baby presses on the bladder.
- Some women experience a surge in energy during this month.
- Dreams about the baby or childbirth may become more vivid.
- Leaking colostrum (early breast milk) from the nipples may begin.

[Health Tips]
- Continue taking prenatal vitamins daily.
- Eat a diet rich in calcium, iron, and protein to support baby's growth.
- Stay hydrated by drinking 8-10 glasses of water daily to reduce swelling and prevent UTIs.
- Practice good posture to alleviate back pain.
- Sleep on your side, preferably the left side, to optimize blood flow to the baby.
- Use pillows between your knees and under your belly for sleeping support.
- Elevate your feet when sitting to reduce ankle and foot swelling.
- Wear supportive, low-heeled shoes with good arch support.
- Apply a moisturizer or oil to the belly to soothe itchy skin (though it won't prevent stretch marks).
- Wear a supportive maternity bra to reduce back strain and breast discomfort.
- Do prenatal exercises like pelvic tilts to strengthen your back.
- Practice Kegel exercises to strengthen pelvic floor muscles.
- Engage in safe, moderate exercise like walking, swimming, or prenatal yoga.
- Eat small, frequent meals to manage heartburn and maintain energy.
- Include high-fiber foods to prevent constipation and hemorrhoids.
- Avoid standing or sitting in one position for too long.
- Wear compression stockings if you are on your feet a lot to help with circulation.
- Take warm (not hot) baths to relieve achiness.
- Use a pregnancy support belt to help with back pain and belly support.
- Apply cold compresses to areas with varicose veins for relief.
- Keep a food diary to identify any foods that trigger heartburn.
- Practice deep breathing exercises to manage shortness of breath.
- Stretch your calf muscles before bed to prevent leg cramps.
- Schedule a dental checkup to maintain oral health.
- Get plenty of fresh air and gentle movement to boost mood.
- Limit sodium intake to help manage swelling.
- Pack healthy snacks like nuts, cheese, and fruit for on-the-go nutrition.
- Practice relaxation techniques to prepare for labor and manage stress.
- Read books or listen to podcasts about childbirth and parenting.
- Attend a prenatal class or tour your hospital/birth center.

[Medical Guidance]
- Your prenatal appointments will now be scheduled every 4 weeks.
- Your healthcare provider will measure your fundal height (from pubic bone to top of uterus).
- The doctor will listen to the baby's heartbeat at every appointment.
- You will likely have a glucose screening test for gestational diabetes around 24-28 weeks.
- Your blood pressure will be monitored closely for signs of preeclampsia.
- Your provider may check for anemia with a blood test.
- Discuss any concerning symptoms like severe swelling, persistent headaches, or vision changes.
- Report any significant decrease in fetal movement.
- Inform your doctor if you experience regular, painful contractions.
- Ask about signs of preterm labor to watch for.
- Discuss your birth plan preferences with your provider.
- Get the Tdap vaccine (between 27-36 weeks) to protect your newborn from whooping cough.
- Ask your doctor about recommended weight gain.
- Discuss any travel plans and get clearance from your provider.
- Talk about common discomforts and safe management options.
- Inquire about prenatal testing for Group B Streptococcus (later in pregnancy).
- Ask about the signs of preterm premature rupture of membranes (water breaking).
- Discuss the baby's position and what to expect in the coming months.
- Report any symptoms of depression or extreme anxiety.
- Confirm which over-the-counter medications are safe for common ailments.
- Keep a list of questions for your doctor between visits.

[Precautions]
- Avoid all alcohol, tobacco, and recreational drugs.
- Continue to limit caffeine to under 200 mg per day.
- Avoid raw or undercooked meat, fish, and eggs.
- Avoid unpasteurized dairy products and soft cheeses.
- Avoid high-mercury fish (swordfish, king mackerel, tilefish).
- Avoid changing cat litter to prevent toxoplasmosis.
- Avoid hot tubs, saunas, and very hot baths.
- Avoid activities with a high risk of falling or abdominal impact.
- Do not take any medications or herbal supplements without your doctor's approval.
- Avoid lying flat on your back for long periods (can restrict blood flow).
- Avoid standing up too quickly to prevent dizziness.
- Wear a seatbelt with the lap strap below your belly and the shoulder strap across your chest.
- Avoid heavy lifting; use your legs and not your back if you must lift something.
- Avoid exposure to harsh chemicals and toxic fumes.
- Avoid prolonged exposure to loud noises.
- Avoid dehydration, which can trigger contractions.
- Do not use harsh chemical hair treatments or dyes without consulting your doctor.
- Avoid excessive junk food; focus on nutrient-dense choices.
- Avoid stressful situations and environments when possible.

[When to Call the Doctor]
- Signs of preterm labor: regular contractions, menstrual-like cramps, low backache, pelvic pressure.
- Any vaginal bleeding or a gush of fluid from the vagina.
- Severe or persistent abdominal pain.
- A significant decrease in the baby's movements.
- Severe, persistent headaches.
- Visual disturbances (blurriness, seeing spots).
- Sudden, severe swelling in the hands, feet, or face.
- Pain or burning during urination, which could indicate a UTI.
- Fever of 100.4°F (38°C) or higher.
- Excessive itching all over the body, which could indicate a liver condition.

[FAQ]
- Is it normal to feel so big? Yes, your uterus is growing rapidly to accommodate the baby.
- Why is the baby so active at night? Your movement during the day can rock the baby to sleep; when you're still, they often wake up.
- Can my diet affect the baby? Yes, the baby can taste flavors from your diet in the amniotic fluid.
- Is it safe to travel? Generally, the second trimester is the safest time, but always consult your doctor.
- What is a glucose test? A test to screen for gestational diabetes, a temporary form of diabetes during pregnancy.
- Can sex harm the baby? No, the baby is well-protected by the amniotic sac and uterus.
- Why do I have leg cramps? They can be caused by fatigue, pressure on nerves, or possibly calcium/magnesium imbalance.
- What are Braxton Hicks? "Practice" contractions that are irregular and don't dilate the cervix.
